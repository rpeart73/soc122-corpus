Through Two-Eyed Seeing

Two-Eyed Seeing here means refusing the habit of treating Indigenous knowledge as a supplement to a Western default. The Indigenous eye sees nations with their own laws, science, governance, and relationships to land that long predate Canada. The Western eye brings the documentary and statistical record. Both are needed to see clearly.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Outline who was here before Canada: the diversity of Indigenous nations, their systems of governance and knowledge, and the relationship to land. Connect to the historical origins of social science as a Western project. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Work with the Martin and Brunette-Debassige sources on what Indigenous ways of knowing are and how Canadian institutions are now trying to make room for them. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students locate one concept from the week on their Cartography and note whether it sits under one eye or both. Personal Cartography 1 is due this week.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the social science useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students add a foundation layer to their Cartography: one thing about Indigenous Canada they did not know they did not know. The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: if the study of Canadian society begins with Indigenous peoples rather than with European arrival, which of your assumptions has to move?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
