Week 12: The Family: Structure, Kinship, and Social Change
Fall 2026

Where This Week Sits

This course moves from naming the social sciences and the Two-Eyed Seeing frame, through the foundation of Indigenous Canada and the methods of social research, into the disciplines themselves, and back to the personal map you draw and then revisit. This week studies the family as a social institution and as lived relationship. We look at how family structures change over time, how kinship and household patterns carry care and responsibility, and how colonial policy, migration, work, gender, and generation shape family life.

Insight

The central question is: How do family structures change over time, and what becomes visible when the social sciences study family as relationship, institution, and lived responsibility?

Through Two-Eyed Seeing

The Western social science eye gives us concepts such as social institution, socialization, kinship, household, role, norm, and change over time. Indigenous ways of knowing keep family connected to relationship, responsibility, land, community, ancestors, and future generations. Two-Eyed Seeing helps us study family without reducing it to one model or treating one framework as the default.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Introduce family as a social institution: kinship, household, care, role expectations, socialization, and changing family forms, using the OpenStax "Marriage and Family" chapter. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Examine how family structures are shaped by culture, land, migration, policy, gender, generation, and community responsibility through the course's Two-Eyed Seeing frame. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students apply one family-structure concept to a public example, fictional case, or course scenario and identify what changes when family is treated as both institution and relationship. Personal Cartography 3 (final) and Knowledge Check 3 (cumulative, take-home) are due this week.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the social science useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students map one family change across three lenses: structure, relationship, and social change. They may use public or fictional examples. No personal disclosure is required. The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: when a society calls one family form normal, what becomes invisible?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
