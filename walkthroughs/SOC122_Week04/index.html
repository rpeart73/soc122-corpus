Through Two-Eyed Seeing

The Western eye brings the documentary record, the Commission's findings, and policy responses. The Indigenous eye brings testimony, survivor knowledge, and the lived continuity of communities. Two-Eyed Seeing treats reconciliation as relationship work that requires both the record and the relationship.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Outline the residential school system, the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, and the Calls to Action, with accuracy and care. Connect to the power nexus of social relations. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Engage with the Smylie and colleagues argument that Indigenous health inequities cannot be closed until data systems are owned and shaped by Indigenous communities, as a present day example of why reconciliation is unfinished and concrete. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students connect one Call to Action to a concept already on their Cartography. No graded assessment is due this week.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the social science useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students write a short, evidence based response connecting a present day example to the history, without speaking for anyone else's experience. The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: what is the difference between feeling sorry about a history and being responsible to the people still living its effects?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
