Through Two-Eyed Seeing

The Western anthropological eye developed the concept of culture and, painfully, the practice of cultural relativism after a colonial history of ranking peoples. The Indigenous eye reminds us that the peoples anthropology once studied have always had their own complete accounts of who they are. Two-Eyed Seeing reads culture from inside and outside at once.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Define culture, cultural relativism, and ethnocentrism using the OpenStax anthropology text. Briefly trace anthropology's colonial origins and its self correction. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Examine a cultural practice through both an outsider description and an insider account, and discuss what each reveals and conceals. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students identify one assumption they hold that is cultural rather than universal, and place it on their Cartography. Knowledge Check 1 is due this week.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the social science useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students describe one everyday practice twice: once as an outsider would and once as an insider would. The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: when you call something normal, whose normal are you describing?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
