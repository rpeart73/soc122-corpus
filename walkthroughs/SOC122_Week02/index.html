Through Two-Eyed Seeing

Two-Eyed Seeing, or Etuaptmumk in Mi'kmaw, is the guiding principle brought forward by Albert Marshall, a Mi'kmaw Elder of Eskasoni who coined the term. It means learning to see with one eye the strengths of Indigenous ways of knowing and with the other eye the strengths of Western ways of knowing, and learning to use both eyes together for the benefit of all. This week treats Two-Eyed Seeing as its own frame: not Indigenous knowledge checked against Western science, but two complete and rigorous ways of knowing held side by side. Little Bear helps explain why the two eyes differ at the root, since Indigenous and Eurocentric worldviews begin from different ideas about reality, time, and relationship.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

Think about it

What is Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk), and why does Albert Marshall describe it as a journey of co-learning rather than a slogan?

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Introduce Two-Eyed Seeing in Elder Albert Marshall's own words, and name Etuaptmumk as a Mi'kmaw concept that he coined. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Read Albert Marshall on the guiding principle, then read Little Bear on why Indigenous and Eurocentric worldviews differ at the root, so the frame comes from Indigenous scholars rather than a paraphrase. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students name one example where the two eyes would see the same situation differently, and say what each eye adds.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the frame useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students start a one page note that answers: what does each eye see well, and what does it tend to miss on its own? The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: whose way of knowing has been treated as the default in the classrooms you have sat in, and who decided that?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
