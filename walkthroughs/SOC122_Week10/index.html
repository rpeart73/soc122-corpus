Through Two-Eyed Seeing

The Western psychological eye gives us experiment, measurement, and theories of mind and behaviour. The Indigenous eye, following Joseph Gone, reframes mental health through historical trauma and on Indigenous terms, naming what individual focused psychology can miss. Two-Eyed Seeing reads the person as both individual and relational.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Introduce psychology's scope, major perspectives, and methods, using the OpenStax psychology text. Note how much psychology was built on a narrow sample of humanity. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Examine a familiar psychological concept and ask how a relational, community centered view would extend or correct it. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students apply one psychological concept to an everyday situation and note what context it leaves out.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the social science useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students explain one psychological concept in plain language and name one context it overlooks. The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: when psychology describes the mind, whose minds were actually studied to build that description?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
