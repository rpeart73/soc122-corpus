Through Two-Eyed Seeing

The Western eye studies identity through development, social influence, and self concept. The Indigenous eye holds identity as inseparable from land, language, kinship, and continuity across generations. Two-Eyed Seeing reads well being as both psychological and relational.

Insight

The frame works only when both ways of knowing remain visible and neither is treated as an add-on.

The Week in Three Moves

Act I (Historical Context, Setting the Stage): Introduce identity development, social influence, and well being, using the OpenStax psychology text. Connect to earlier sociology on socialization. Act II (Media, Primary Scene): Discuss how identity is shaped by belonging and culture, drawing on the cultural safety idea from Week 5 and the relational view from Week 10. Act III (Discussion, Application): Students map one part of their own identity to the relationships and contexts that shaped it. Knowledge Check 2 is due this week.

Insight

The week is built as a path: set the context, work with sources, then apply the idea in a bounded way.

Practice Without Over-Disclosure

This week uses a short lecture frame, source guided reading, key concepts, guided notes, and one low stakes practice. The goal is to make the social science useful and to keep both eyes open, without turning the week into a personal disclosure task. The practice is: Students name one relationship or context that shaped a part of their identity, kept general and non private. The reflection question is: A question to keep, not to answer: which parts of who you are would not exist without the people and places that made them?

Insight

The practice asks for careful application, not private disclosure. Use public, fictional, or general examples when that is the safer choice.
